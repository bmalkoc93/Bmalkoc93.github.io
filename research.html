Rebuilding Precarity - Accra, Ghana

My master's research uses an urban political ecology framework to understand the complexities that face an informal community in Accra, Ghana. Residents of the beach at Jamestown are the targets of ongoing demolitions by the city municipal government to make way for a new Chinese-backed harbor. Hundreds of dwellings were destroyed, although many residents kept returning to the beach to rebuild their dwellings. I conducted two months of qualitative fieldwork, including semi-structured interviews and a participatory drawing method which I designed, to study how residents rebuilt smaller dwellings with less expensive materials. In understanding how peoples' dwellings changed, this research contributes to a deeper understanding of the issues that vulnerbale communities face. Unequal access to resources (especially of fish due to foreign overfishing in West Africa) limited how residents could construct their dwellings, while living in anticipation of future demolitions forced people to make short-term decisions that led to increased precarity.

Desertification - Kalahari, Botswana

My undergraduate honors thesis analyzed data I collected on soil organic carbon and nitrogen content of soil samples from the Kalahari desert while working as a research assistant at UCLA. I conducted a spatial analysis of the relationship between grazing zones, vegetation coverage, and SOC and N content, however, I did not find a statistically significant correlation or spatial difference between sample points. I concluded that local knowledge of grazing conditions and desert vegetation in the Kalahari favors normal soil conditions as opposed to previous desertification research which implicates pastoralism as a primary contributor to land degradation of deserts.
