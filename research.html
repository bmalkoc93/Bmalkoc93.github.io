    Current Research

    Rebuilding Precarity - Accra, Ghana

    My current research builds off an urban political ecology framework to understand how an informal community in Accra, Ghana is unequally impacted by infrastructure development. Residents of the beach at Jamestown are the targets of ongoing demolitions by the city municipal government to make way for a new Chinese-backed harbor. Hundreds of dwellings were destroyed, yet many residents kept returning to the beach to rebuild their dwellings. Drawing on two months of qualitative fieldwork including semi-structured interviews and a participatory drawing method, this research studies the design and materials of residents' rebuilt dwellings changed in anticipation of future demolitions. This work addresses how impacts to human security are multiplied by development patterns that prevent effective climate change adaptation.

    Past Research

    Desertification - Kalahari, Botswana

    My undergraduate honors thesis analyzes data I collected on soil organic carbon and nitrogen content of soil samples from the Kalahari desert while working as a research assistant under Dr. Gregory Okin at UCLA. I conducted a spatial analysis of the relationship between grazing zones, vegetation coverage, and SOC and N content, however, I did not find a statistically significant correlation or spatial difference between sample points. I concluded that local knowledge of grazing conditions and desert vegetation in the Kalahari favors normal soil conditions as opposed to previous desertification research which implicates pastoralism as a primary contributor to land degradation of deserts.
